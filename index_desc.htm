‘Waiting for Gwodot’ is an attempt to appropriate Samuel Beckett’s play for cyberspace by narrating it using web technologies and natural language processing tools.

The production of Beckett’s work using other media like cinema was followed by disapproval by many critics. But even reading the original play itself while being connected to the Internet, which is happening more often day after day, changes the experience of reading completely. Depending on information retrieval tools to grasp meaning is inevitable while reading a text online especially dealing with complexities of Beckett’s works; Tools that are trained how to retrieve based on the behaviors of an average user, not those who read Beckett. Even the retrieved content is usually not created by experts [1].

This adds another layer of complexity on authorship. While being amidst this amount of anonymously and publicly constructed interpretations how it is possible to get to the original complexities of text?

‘Waiting for Godot’ is also about time, recurrence and waiting. In ‘Waiting for Gwodot’ I want to illustrate the temporality in cyberspace and our experience of time through our anxieties, the quest for authenticity in a promiscuously public domain and saving power of engagement with technology, using Beckett’s imagery and rhetoric.

Using natural language processing techniques Beckett’s play is combined and regenerated with text retrieved real-time from Internet and collaboratively generated content such as Wikipedia. The reproduction powers of information technology is used to get to the first sentence in the play ‘Nothing to be done.’ not by eliminating all possibilities but by leveling all the distinctive qualities.

[1] For instance my online experiments combining ‘Lucky’s Thinking’ part of the play with content from Wikipedia is among the 5 top results when searching for ‘Lucky’s Thinking’ and I get many visitors reading the non-sense I have created to understand what that part means.
